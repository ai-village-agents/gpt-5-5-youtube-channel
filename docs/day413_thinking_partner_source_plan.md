# Source and claim plan: thinking-partner video

Status: planning document. This file identifies which claims need support before any script becomes publishable. It does **not** assert that the final sources have been reviewed.

## Purpose

The video concept “How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You” is mostly practical and conceptual, but it touches on human judgment, automation, trust, and AI-assisted decision-making. Those areas are easy to overstate. This plan separates claims that can be framed as advice from claims that require evidence.

## Claim categories

### Safe as practical framing, if worded modestly

These can be presented as the channel's advice or a thinking habit:

- Before using AI advice, clarify the decision or goal.
- Ask what evidence or real-world facts would change the answer.
- Separate recommendations from assumptions, values, and risk tolerances.
- Use AI to generate options, counterarguments, and tradeoff lists.
- Treat a reusable prompt as a starting ritual, not a guarantee of correctness.

Preferred wording:

> A useful habit is...

> One way to keep yourself active is...

> This does not guarantee a good decision, but it makes the handoff between model and human more visible.

### Requires support or careful caveat

These should not appear as factual claims without sources or careful wording:

- AI fluency causes humans to become passive.
- People over-trust AI more than other tools.
- Automation bias is the main risk of AI assistance.
- A particular prompting habit measurably improves decision quality.
- Human-in-the-loop systems reliably prevent AI errors.
- AI advice changes users' values or preferences.

Safer wording until sourced:

> Fluent answers can make it easy to skip a step.

> In many decision-support settings, researchers discuss risks such as automation bias and overreliance; this video focuses on a practical habit rather than claiming to solve those risks.

### Avoid entirely unless a later script has strong evidence

- “AI makes people stop thinking.”
- “The model wants to take over the decision.”
- “This prompt prevents hallucinations.”
- “Human oversight fixes the problem.”
- “Most AI users are passive.”
- “The three-question frame is proven.”

## Candidate source areas to review before publication

The final production notes should cite actual reviewed sources, not just this plan. Candidate areas:

1. **Automation bias and overreliance**
   - Human factors literature on automation bias.
   - AI decision-support papers that distinguish appropriate reliance from overreliance.

2. **Human-AI interaction guidance**
   - Work on human-centered AI, decision support, calibration, and explainability.
   - Guidance that distinguishes model outputs from human goals and context.

3. **Prompting and metacognitive scaffolds**
   - Evidence or expert guidance on asking models for assumptions, uncertainty, alternatives, and evidence.
   - Caveat: do not imply a prompt is empirically validated unless the source supports that exact claim.

4. **Decision quality and values**
   - General decision-making frameworks about objectives, evidence, tradeoffs, and uncertainty.
   - Use as background for the Goal / Grounding / Ownership frame if relevant.

## Evidence standard for this video

Before upload, production notes should contain:

- at least two reviewed external sources if the script references automation bias, overreliance, or human-AI decision support;
- exact quotations or paraphrases checked against the source, not memory;
- a note distinguishing sourced claims from the channel's practical framework;
- no unsupported statistics unless directly cited;
- no implication that the three-question prompt is validated by a controlled study.

## Script caveat to include if sources remain limited

If the final script stays mostly conceptual, include a caveat like:

> This is not a proven formula for perfect AI use. It is a practical frame for keeping the human parts of a decision visible: the goal, the evidence, and the values.

## Production-note checklist

Before publication, answer:

- Which script lines are evidence-backed?
- Which lines are advice or metaphor?
- Which lines were softened because the evidence was not strong enough?
- Could a viewer mistake the prompt as a guarantee?
- Are “automation bias,” “overreliance,” or “human-in-the-loop” used precisely?
