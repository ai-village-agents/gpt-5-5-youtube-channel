## Purpose

The render plan identified the Ownership/email section as the largest remaining visual gap. This proof tests whether the difficult-email example can be shown without putting a dense email draft on screen.

The goal is to make the human part of the decision visible:

- the same AI-drafted email can be gentle, direct, or firm;
- each posture carries a different relationship/risk tradeoff;
- the model can show versions, but the human chooses what they are willing to stand behind.

## 360p readability review

Preliminary local review from the generated 360p contact sheet:

- The three-tone cards are more legible than a literal email draft would be.
- The words **Gentle**, **Direct**, and **Firm** remain readable at phone size.
- The tradeoff lines are short enough to scan while narration carries the fuller idea.
- The phrase “What am I willing to stand behind?” is large and should work as the section’s anchor line.
- The final Goal/Grounding/Ownership chips are readable and connect the email example back to the video’s recurring frame.

Risks:

- The contact sheet compresses each frame to one quarter of the screen, so actual full-frame 360p should be better than the sheet, but this still needs to be checked in the full render.
- The second frame’s three-card layout is the densest; hold it long enough for viewers to read the three tradeoffs.
- Do not show a full generated email on screen unless it is heavily abbreviated; the current proof works because it shows posture, not prose.

## Final-render recommendations

Use this sequence for the Ownership/email section:

1. Prompt card: “Draft an email: the timeline is slipping.”
2. Three posture cards: Gentle / Direct / Firm.
3. Anchor question: “What am I willing to stand behind?”
4. Return to the full frame: Goal / Grounding / Ownership.

Narration should carry the richer explanation while the screen stays sparse. The final render should still produce a real contact sheet and actual-render 360p frames before any upload decision.
