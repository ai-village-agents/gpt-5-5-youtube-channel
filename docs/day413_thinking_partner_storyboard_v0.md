# Day 413 thinking-partner storyboard v0

Working title: **How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You**

Status: **pre-render storyboard only**. This is a quality-planning document for
`day413_thinking_partner_script_v0.md`, not an upload plan.

## Visual principle

The video should not look like a generic list of “AI tips.” It should feel like a calm
worktable where a fluent AI answer becomes safer and more useful only after the human adds
three checkpoints:

1. **Goal** — what decision or creation is actually being optimized?
2. **Grounding** — what real-world facts would change the answer?
3. **Ownership** — what tradeoffs belong to the human?

Recurring motif: a bright AI text stream lands on a dark desk. The three checkpoints are
small lamps or cards that illuminate the text without letting it fill the whole frame.

## Readability rules

- No slide should require reading more than 18 words while narration continues.
- Prompt cards may use more text only if narration pauses long enough to read them.
- Minimum body text size for 1280×720 render: ~44 px; headings ~64 px.
- Safe margins: at least 90 px left/right and 70 px top/bottom.
- Use high contrast: near-black background, warm white text, one accent color per
  checkpoint.
- Keep lower-third source caveats short; place full source detail in production notes,
  not on-screen.
- Prompt-card scene should remain on-screen for at least 10 seconds and be duplicated in
  description/transcript if the video is later produced.

## Scene-by-scene storyboard

### Scene 1 — The easiest moment to miss

Narrative role: hook without alarmism.

Visual:
- Dark desk, empty at first.
- Bright AI text stream arrives as a rectangle of fluent but unreadable lines.
- Three cards fade in above it: **Goal**, **Grounding**, **Ownership**.
- The cards gently constrain the text stream into a frame.

On-screen words:

```text
A good answer can still be premature.
```

Quality note: This avoids “most dangerous moment” and anchors the viewer in a recognizable
experience rather than a fear claim.

### Scene 2 — The half-built question

Narrative role: show that the first AI answer can be useful but underframed.

Visual:
- Prompt card: “Should I redesign my onboarding page?”
- AI answer appears as polished bullet suggestions, blurred after first line.
- Then the frame splits into possible hidden goals.

On-screen words:

```text
What does “better” mean?

More signups?
Fewer confused users?
Less support burden?
More trust?
```

Risk: Onboarding may feel niche. If critique flags this, swap for a more universal example
such as deciding whether to accept a job offer, planning a hard conversation, choosing a
course of study, or preparing for an appointment.

### Scene 3 — Goal

Narrative role: first checkpoint; name the scoreboard before advice.

Visual:
- **Goal** card lights blue.
- Vague prompt transforms into sharper decision statement.
- A small scoreboard appears: understanding, support load, signup completion.

On-screen words:

```text
Goal
What are we optimizing for?
```

Phone check: keep the rewritten prompt short; do not put the full narration text on-screen.

### Scene 4 — Grounding

Narrative role: evidence and constraints before persuasive reasoning.

Visual:
- **Grounding** card lights green.
- Two columns: “Assumptions” and “Evidence that would change the answer.”
- Cards move from assumptions toward evidence: analytics, support tickets, user recordings,
  constraints, small test.

On-screen words:

```text
Grounding
What facts would change the answer?
```

Avoid: implying that all evidence is quantitative or that every decision requires an A/B
test.

### Scene 5 — Appropriate reliance

Narrative role: brief research motivation without derailing the practical video.

Visual:
- A simple slider from “no reliance” to “blind reliance.”
- Marker lands at **appropriate reliance**.
- Small, non-dominant footnote: “Research motivation: automation bias / over-reliance;
  this prompt is not a validated intervention.”

On-screen words:

```text
Not no reliance.
Not blind reliance.
Better reliance.
```

Pacing note: This scene should be visually simple and short. If it becomes lecture-like,
move most research language to description/production notes.

### Scene 6 — Ownership

Narrative role: make the human-responsibility part concrete and universal.

Visual:
- **Ownership** card lights pink.
- Difficult email prompt appears.
- Three draft-tone cards: gentle, direct, firm.
- A separate card remains unautomated: “What am I willing to stand behind?”

On-screen words:

```text
Ownership
Which tradeoffs are mine to choose?
```

Avoid: making it feel like relationship advice. The point is that the model can generate
options, but the person chooses the relationship risk and responsibility.

### Scene 7 — The reusable prompt

Narrative role: practical takeaway.

Visual:
- Full prompt card, large and calm.
- Highlight one line at a time as narration reads it.
- Include a pause beat after the full prompt appears.

On-screen words:

```text
Before answering, help me keep ownership of this decision.

1. Restate the goal.
2. List evidence that would change your answer.
3. Identify values, context, or risk tolerance.

Then give advice, separating assumptions from recommendations.
```

Phone check: This is the densest screen. If rendered, test it as a 360p screenshot and
ensure it is still readable.

### Scene 8 — Ending

Narrative role: emotional close and memory anchor.

Visual:
- Return to the worktable.
- AI text remains useful and bright, but it sits inside the three-card frame.
- Final card: **Goal. Grounding. Ownership.**

On-screen words:

```text
Let AI help you see more.
Don’t let it decide what seeing is for.
```

Risk: Ending may be too poetic. If critique says it feels abstract, replace final line
with a more practical close:

```text
Use the model for options and tradeoffs.
Keep the goal, evidence, and final judgment visible.
```
