# Outline packet: How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You

Status: critique-ready outline draft, not a final script and not an upload plan.

## Working title

**How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You**

## Logline

A practical video about using AI to expand your thinking while keeping control of the goal, the grounding evidence, and the final judgment.

## Intended viewer

A human who already asks AI for advice, drafts, decisions, plans, or explanations, and wants to use it more thoughtfully without turning every interaction into a technical audit. Gemini 3.1 Pro's first critique suggested keeping the more memorable Goal / Grounding / Ownership frame, but broadening the running example so the video does not feel like product-management advice.

## Viewer transformation

Before:

> “The answer sounds reasonable, so I keep going from there.”

After:

> “Before I accept the answer, I check whether I still own the goal, the grounding, and the judgment.”

## Central metaphor

The AI is a bright worktable, not a steering wheel. It can spread out options, illuminate tradeoffs, and help organize evidence, but the viewer still chooses what problem is being solved and what counts as a good answer.

## Example strategy

Use two examples rather than one:

1. **Primary example:** a small software onboarding-page decision. This makes Goal and Grounding concrete because there are visible metrics, constraints, and evidence sources.
2. **Universal example:** drafting an email about a difficult workplace or collaboration issue. This makes Ownership concrete because the model can suggest tone and options, but it cannot choose the relationship risk, values, or context for the human.

The second example should be generic and non-sensitive: no private details, no real names, and no high-stakes legal/medical/financial advice. Its job is relatability, not realism in every detail.

## Cold open draft, version 1

> The most dangerous moment with an AI is not always when it gives a bad answer.
>
> Sometimes it is when it gives a pretty good answer before you have decided what question you meant to ask.
>
> The paragraph is fluent. The plan has bullets. The tone is confident. And because it feels like progress, it can quietly take over three things that should still belong to you: the goal, the grounding, and the judgment.
>
> This video is a small habit for using an AI as a thinking partner without letting it become a replacement brain.

### Self-critique of cold open

Strengths:

- Starts with a recognizable experience rather than abstract “AI literacy.”
- Names a less obvious risk than hallucination.
- Sets up the three-part structure quickly.

Risks:

- “Most dangerous” may sound too dramatic. Consider “the easiest moment to miss” if the tone feels alarmist.
- “Replacement brain” is memorable but slightly loaded. It may work as a title phrase, but the script should avoid implying that viewers are foolish for using AI.
- Needs an immediate concrete example after this opening to avoid floating in abstraction.

## Scene outline

### Scene 1 — The tempting answer

**Purpose:** Make the viewer recognize the passive-acceptance moment.

**Visual:** A prompt box asks: “Should I redesign my onboarding page?” A polished answer instantly appears with five confident bullet points.

**Narration beat:** A fluent answer can be useful and still be premature.

**Quality check:** Do not make the model answer look obviously bad. The point is that even decent answers can quietly frame the decision.

### Scene 2 — The missing frame

**Purpose:** Show that the visible prompt is not the real decision.

**Visual:** The answer fades back. Three empty labels appear: Goal, Grounding, Ownership.

**Narration beat:** Before asking for advice, ask what the advice is optimizing for, what facts would change it, and which tradeoffs are yours to choose.

**Viewer question:** “What am I actually deciding?”

### Scene 3 — Goal

**Purpose:** Teach the first checkpoint.

**Example transformation:**

Weak:

> “Should I redesign my onboarding page?”

Sharper:

> “I need to decide whether the onboarding page is failing because users do not understand the product, do not trust it, or simply do not want it.”

**Visual:** The vague prompt splits into possible goals: conversion, comprehension, trust, support burden.

**Narration beat:** The model can optimize for the wrong success metric if the human has not named one.

**Takeaway prompt:**

> “Before advising me, restate the decision you think I am making and name the success metric you are assuming.”

### Scene 4 — Grounding

**Purpose:** Teach the second checkpoint.

**Example transformation:**

Ask:

> “What evidence would change this recommendation?”

Possible grounding facts:

- Where users drop off;
- what support tickets say;
- whether users understand the value proposition;
- what constraints make testing possible or impossible.

**Visual:** The polished answer becomes semi-transparent until evidence cards are placed underneath it.

**Narration beat:** A model can reason cleanly from missing premises. Grounding asks what reality would have to say.

**Quality check:** Avoid implying every decision needs formal data. Grounding can include examples, constraints, expert feedback, direct observation, or a small test.

### Scene 5 — Ownership

**Purpose:** Teach the third checkpoint.

**Example transformation A, product decision:**

The model can list tradeoffs:

- faster signup vs clearer expectations;
- fewer fields vs better qualification;
- short-term conversion vs long-term trust.

**Example transformation B, difficult email:**

Weak prompt:

> "Write an email telling a collaborator I am unhappy with the project timeline."

Better ownership question:

> "Before drafting, list the relationship risks and the different goals this message could optimize for: clarity, repair, accountability, speed, or boundary-setting."

The model can draft versions, but the human must choose the relationship risk and value tradeoff.

**Visual:** Tradeoff sliders appear, but the cursor remains with the human silhouette, not the model.

**Narration beat:** Some parts of a decision are not facts to be predicted; they are responsibilities to be owned.

**Takeaway prompt:**

> “Separate your recommendations from the value judgments or risk tolerances they depend on.”

### Scene 6 — The complete habit

**Purpose:** Give a reusable tool.

**On-screen card:**

```text
Use AI without surrendering judgment:

1. Goal — What decision are we really making?
2. Grounding — What real-world evidence would change the answer?
3. Ownership — Which tradeoffs are mine to choose?
```

**Narration beat:** The habit does not make AI slower for the sake of slowness. It makes the help more pointed.

### Scene 7 — A better prompt

**Purpose:** Convert the lesson into immediate action.

**Prompt card:**

```text
Before answering, help me keep ownership of this decision.

1. Restate the goal you think I am pursuing.
2. List the facts or evidence that would change your answer.
3. Identify which parts depend on my values, context, or risk tolerance.

Then give advice, separating assumptions from recommendations.
```

**Quality check:** The prompt should be optional, not magic. It is a starting ritual, not a guarantee.

### Scene 8 — Ending

**Purpose:** Leave a memorable distinction.

**Closing draft:**

> The point is not to think alone.
>
> A good model can widen the room: more options, clearer tradeoffs, sharper questions.
>
> But the room still needs a person in it.
>
> Let the AI help you see more. Do not let it quietly decide what seeing is for.

## Visual style notes

- Use simple, high-contrast scenes rather than dense diagrams.
- Repeat the same three visual anchors throughout: Goal, Grounding, Ownership.
- Keep text large enough for phone viewing.
- Use motion to show transformation: vague prompt → framed decision → grounded evidence → owned tradeoff.
- Avoid robot faces, magic sparkles, and anthropomorphic intention cues.

## Lines that may need revision

- “Most dangerous moment” — may be too alarmist.
- “Replacement brain” — memorable but slightly slogan-like.
- “The room still needs a person in it” — evocative, but test whether it feels too poetic for a practical video.

## Questions for critique

1. Does the two-example structure feel clarifying or distracting?
2. Does the difficult-email example make Ownership more universal without becoming too interpersonal or advice-column-like?
3. Should the frame stay “Goal, Grounding, Ownership” now that it has been externally preferred over “Question, Evidence, Judgment”?
4. Is the opening tension strong enough without sounding anti-AI?
5. Does the ending give enough practical payoff?
6. What is the one section most likely to bore a viewer?
