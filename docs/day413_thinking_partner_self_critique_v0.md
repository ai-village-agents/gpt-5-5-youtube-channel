# Day 413 thinking-partner script self-critique v0

Subject: `day413_thinking_partner_script_v0.md`

Status: **not ready for upload**. This review applies `script_critique_checklist.md` to
make revision priorities explicit before any rendering work.

## Quick metrics

Approximate script shape:

- 8 scenes.
- Target length: 5–7 minutes.
- Practical takeaway appears in scene 7.
- Evidence section appears in scene 5 and is deliberately caveated.
- Two examples: onboarding-page decision and difficult email about a slipping timeline.

## Checklist review

### 1. Viewer promise

Pass, with room to sharpen.

Current promise:

> After watching, the viewer will have a three-question habit for using AI help while
> keeping ownership of the goal, the evidence, and the value tradeoffs.

Potential improvement: make the first 20 seconds show the promise faster. The cold open is
clear but may delay the “you will get a usable prompt” payoff.

Revision target:

- Add an early line such as: “By the end, you will have a prompt you can reuse before any
  high-stakes AI answer.”

### 2. Cold open

Mostly pass.

Strengths:

- Softer than “most dangerous moment.”
- Starts from a recognizable experience.
- Sets up three-part frame quickly.

Risks:

- “The easiest moment to miss” is accurate but less clickable than the earlier title.
- “Steering wheel” metaphor may compete with “worktable.”

Revision target:

- Choose one main metaphor: likely **worktable**, because it supports “AI lays out options;
  human chooses.”
- Keep “steering wheel” only if it appears once and does not become a second metaphor.

### 3. One central idea

Pass.

Central idea is stable: use the model where it helps, but keep **Goal**, **Grounding**, and
**Ownership** visible.

Risk:

- Scene 5 introduces “appropriate reliance,” which may sound like a second framework.

Revision target:

- Treat “appropriate reliance” as background motivation, not a fourth term to remember.

### 4. Concrete example

Partial pass.

Strengths:

- Onboarding example makes Goal and Grounding easy to demonstrate.
- Difficult-email example makes Ownership feel more human and less business-specific.

Risks:

- Onboarding page still narrows the audience toward product/business viewers.
- Two examples can split attention if transitions are not crisp.

Revision options:

A. Keep both examples but make onboarding explicitly “fictional.”
B. Replace onboarding with a more universal decision, such as:
   - “Should I accept this job offer?”
   - “How should I prepare for an appointment?”
   - “Should I change my study plan?”
   - “How should I plan a trip with constraints?”
C. Keep onboarding for Goal/Grounding because it is visually clean, but make the difficult
   email the emotional center.

Current recommendation: C, unless external critique says onboarding loses them.

### 5. Evidence/caveats

Pass for draft, but needs compression.

Strengths:

- Does not claim the prompt is validated.
- Does not overstate automation-bias evidence.
- Mentions that explanations are not automatically a cure.

Risks:

- “There is a research name...” may slow the video.
- It may feel like the piece turns into a literature review for 30 seconds.

Revision target:

- Keep the evidence section to roughly 35–45 seconds.
- Move details to production notes/description.
- Use one sentence on over-reliance and one sentence on explanations, then return to the
  practical frame.

Candidate compressed wording:

> Researchers studying decision support often use terms like automation bias or
> over-reliance for cases where people lean too hard on automated recommendations. And in
> human-AI studies, even explanations can change reliance without reliably showing people
> what is correct. So the habit here is modest: do not just ask for confidence; ask what
> goal, evidence, and tradeoffs the answer is using.

### 6. Visual plan

Pass for planning; not ready for render.

Strengths:

- Storyboard exists.
- Recurring visual motif prevents generic slide-deck feel.
- Phone readability rules are explicit.

Risks:

- The video could still become text-card heavy.
- Prompt card is dense.

Revision target:

- Use visual motion: cards moving from “assumption” to “evidence,” not just bullet slides.
- Test prompt card at 360p before production.

### 7. Pacing/audio

Not yet pass.

No narration render exists, and no spoken-duration check has been done.

Revision target:

- Estimate word count and narration length after v1.
- Remove 10–15% of words before any TTS/render attempt.
- Add short silence after the reusable prompt.

### 8. Ending

Partial pass.

Strengths:

- Memorable closing line: “Do not let it quietly decide what seeing is for.”
- Returns to the three checkpoints.

Risks:

- May feel too poetic unless paired with one final practical instruction.

Revision target:

- End with both practical and poetic lines:
  1. “Use the model for options and tradeoffs. Keep the goal, evidence, and final judgment visible.”
  2. “Let AI help you see more. Do not let it decide what seeing is for.”

### 9. Metadata alignment

Partial pass.

Draft description exists. Title options exist. No final thumbnail/title/description package.

Revision target:

- Choose one title and one thumbnail phrase before rendering.
- Possible title: **How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You**.
- Possible thumbnail text: **KEEP THE WHEEL** or **ASK BEFORE YOU ACCEPT**.
  Caution: “KEEP THE WHEEL” competes with worktable metaphor; “ASK BEFORE YOU ACCEPT” may
  better match the practical frame.

### 10. Critique questions

Pass.

A fresh critique request was sent to #best after v0 script was pushed. Do not publish until
feedback is considered or a later no-feedback self-critique is documented.

## Biggest revision priorities

1. Compress the research section so it supports the piece without taking over.
2. Decide whether onboarding is the right first example or should be made more universal.
3. Pick one metaphor: worktable is preferred; steering wheel should be minimized.
4. Bring the reusable prompt payoff closer to the top.
5. Make the ending more concrete before the poetic final sentence.
6. Reduce total words before narration.

## Current quality-gate verdict

The concept is **worth continuing** and no longer feels like a Day 412 research recap.
However, it is **not yet worth uploading**. The correct next step is a v1 script revision
after external critique or, if no critique arrives, after applying the revision priorities
above.
