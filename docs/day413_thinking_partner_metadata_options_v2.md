## Recommended title

**How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You**

Why this remains the best working title:

- It matches the final line of the script: “Let the model widen the table. Keep your hands on the
  question.”
- It is broad enough for a thoughtful general audience, not only viewers searching for prompt
  engineering.
- It frames the video as collaboration with AI, not refusal to use AI.
- It creates a clear promise that the ending now answers: delegate options and drafts, but keep
  purpose, evidence standard, risk tolerance, and final judgment.

Main risk:

- “Without letting it think for you” could sound moralizing if paired with a fear-based thumbnail.
  The opening and metadata should emphasize “thinking partner” and “keep the decision visible.”

## Preferred description draft

A good AI answer can still arrive too early.

This video gives you one reusable prompt for using AI as a thinking partner while keeping three
human checkpoints visible: **Goal**, **Evidence**, and **Ownership**.

Use the model for options, tradeoffs, sharper questions, and first drafts. Keep the purpose, evidence
standard, risk tolerance, and final judgment where you can see them.

Reusable prompt from the video:

```text
Before answering, help me keep ownership of this decision.

1. Restate the goal you think I am pursuing.
2. List the facts or evidence that would change your answer.
3. Identify which parts depend on my values, context, or risk tolerance.

Then give advice, separating assumptions from recommendations.
```

This is a practical scaffold, not a validated safety intervention. The research context is
human-factors and human-AI decision-support work on over-reliance, automation bias, and appropriate
reliance.

Sources and context:
- Skitka, Mosier, & Burdick (1999), “Does automation bias decision-making?” DOI:
  10.1006/ijhc.1999.0252
- Decision Support and Automation Bias review (2011), DOI: 10.3233/978-1-60750-709-3-3
- “Explanations, Fairness, and Appropriate Reliance in Human-AI Decision-Making” DOI:
  10.1145/3613904.3642621 / arXiv: 2209.11812

## Warmer shorter description option

Sometimes the problem is not that an AI answer is bad. It is that the answer arrives before the
question is finished.

This video is about a small habit for slowing the first answer down: ask the model to restate the
goal, name the evidence that would change the answer, and separate advice from the parts that depend
on your values, context, or risk tolerance.

Goal. Evidence. Ownership.

Use AI to widen the table: more options, clearer tradeoffs, sharper questions. Keep the final
judgment with you.

This is a practical scaffold, not a validated safety intervention. It is motivated by
decision-support research on over-reliance, automation bias, and appropriate reliance.

## Description caveats to preserve

Include at least one of these sentences in any final upload description:

- “This is a practical scaffold, not a validated safety intervention.”
- “This prompt does not prove that an AI answer is correct.”
- “The goal is appropriate reliance, not avoiding AI help.”
- “Use the model for options and tradeoffs; keep the human judgment visible.”

Do **not** claim:

- the prompt prevents over-reliance;
- the prompt prevents hallucinations;
- explanations are useless;
- AI makes people passive;
- human oversight solves automation bias;
- the cited studies prove this exact prompt works;
- the captions, thumbnail, cards, or public endpoint checks are complete before they are actually
  completed in Studio and logged.
