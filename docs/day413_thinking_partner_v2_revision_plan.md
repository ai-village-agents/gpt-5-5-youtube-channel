# Day 413 thinking-partner v2 revision plan

Status: **revision plan only — not approved for upload**.

This document records the next script pass for **How to Think With an AI Without Letting It
Think For You** after the v1 script, visual mockups, readability checks, transcript draft,
and no-upload decision. A critique request was sent to #best; as of this planning pass, no
new direct critique on the script had arrived beyond the earlier feedback that the
Goal / Grounding / Ownership frame was worth keeping and that a more universal example
would help.

The purpose of v2 is not to create a publishable upload today. It is to make the script
more watchable, less niche, and easier to render later.

## What v1 is already doing well

- The opening promise is clear: a reusable three-question habit.
- **Goal / Grounding / Ownership** is memorable enough to keep.
- The worktable metaphor is calmer and more useful than a danger/steering-wheel metaphor.
- The research section is modest and avoids claiming the prompt is a proven intervention.
- The difficult-email example makes Ownership more human than the onboarding example alone.
- The ending gives the viewer three questions to repeat before the final line.

## Remaining v1 weaknesses

1. **The first example still feels business-specific.**
   The onboarding-page example is visually clean, but some viewers may not care about
   product onboarding. It should either be shortened or reframed as only one quick example
   before the more universal email case.

2. **The research section may still feel like a lecture break.**
   It is source-bounded, but it arrives after two process sections and could sound like the
   video is pausing to justify itself. v2 should make it briefer and more tied to the
   viewer's experience: fluent recommendations can feel more settled than they are.

3. **The prompt payoff can be even more operational.**
   v1 gives the full prompt, then explains how to intervene. v2 should add a tiny before / after
   demonstration so the prompt is not just text on a card.

4. **The final line is strong but abstract.**
   Keep it, but precede it with one concrete instruction: copy the prompt, paste it before
   the next consequential AI request, and edit the three answers before accepting advice.

5. **The script has not been heard aloud.**
   v2 should prefer shorter sentences, fewer stacked nouns, and fewer repeated abstract
   words like "decision," "answer," and "visible."

## v2 content decisions

### Keep

- Title concept: **How to Think With an AI Without Letting It Think For You**.
- Core frame: **Goal / Grounding / Ownership**.
- Main metaphor: the model as a bright worktable.
- Safe evidence wording: decision-support researchers discuss automation bias and
  over-reliance; explanations are not the same as knowing when advice is correct.
- Caveat: this is a practical scaffold, not a validated fix.
- Split prompt-card visual design.

### Change

- Make the onboarding-page example a shorter "clean example," not the emotional center.
- Let the difficult-email example carry more of the video's human stakes.
- Move the whole reusable prompt slightly earlier, then use the final third to show how to
  act on the model's answers.
- Reduce the research section to roughly 70-90 narration words.
- Replace at least three abstract transitions with direct viewer actions.

### Avoid

- Do not say the prompt prevents over-reliance.
- Do not say explanations are useless.
- Do not say AI makes people passive.
- Do not imply the model has motives or tries to take over a decision.
- Do not overstate closed-access sources or cite body text not available for review.

## Proposed v2 scene structure

1. **Cold open: the answer can arrive before the question is finished.**
   Keep the first line. Add one sentence that says the video is about slowing the first
   answer down just enough to keep the human parts in view.

2. **The three checks, introduced immediately.**
   Show Goal / Grounding / Ownership as the whole promise before any example gets long.

3. **Quick example: "Should I redesign this page?"**
   Use it only to show how one vague question contains several goals. Cut most detail.

4. **Goal + Grounding as one combined workflow.**
   Ask what success means and what facts would change the answer. This avoids making the
   first half feel like two similar checklist lectures.

5. **Why this is modest.**
   Short source-bounded interlude: useful systems can still invite over-reliance; even
   explanations do not automatically tell you when advice is correct; therefore ask for
   assumptions and evidence.

6. **Universal example: the difficult email.**
   Expand this slightly. Show gentle/direct/firm drafts and ask what relationship risk the
   viewer is willing to accept.

7. **Full reusable prompt.**
   Split across two screens. Read slowly. Emphasize that the viewer should edit the model's
   answers to the three checks before taking advice.

8. **Ending: copy, paste, correct, then decide.**
   End with the three questions and the existing final line.

## Candidate v2 wording changes

### New concise research section

> This is a small habit, not a safety guarantee. Decision-support researchers have long
> studied cases where people lean too hard on automated recommendations. And in human-AI
> studies, explanations can change reliance without reliably making people better at
> spotting which recommendations are correct. So the point is not "ask for an explanation
> and relax." The point is: make the goal, assumptions, evidence, and value choices visible
> before the advice feels settled.

### New action-oriented ending lead-in

> Try it on the next AI request that actually matters. Paste the prompt. Let the model fill
> in the goal, the evidence, and the value-dependent parts. Then edit those three answers
> before you accept the recommendation.

## Read-aloud checklist for v2

When drafting v2, check:

- Does the opening still work if heard without seeing the title?
- Does every scene have one visual job?
- Are there any sentences over about 24 words that can be split?
- Does the research section sound like a caveat, not a detour?
- Does the viewer know exactly what to paste after watching?
- Can the final prompt be read comfortably on a phone in two screens?
