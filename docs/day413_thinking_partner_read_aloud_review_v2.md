## Longest sentence checks

The longest narration sentence is 23 words:

> If it says the choice depends on values, context, or risk tolerance, do not outsource
> that part just because the prose is smooth.

This is acceptable but should be tested aloud. It has several abstractions in one breath:
values, context, risk tolerance, outsource, prose. A possible shorter version:

> If the choice depends on values, context, or risk tolerance, pause. Smooth prose cannot
> choose that for you.

Other sentences around 19-21 words are mostly list sentences. They are understandable, but
v3 should cut or split at least the following:

1. Onboarding recommendation list:
   > The model might answer instantly: simplify the page, reduce friction, emphasize the
   > main benefit, add social proof, test a clearer button.

   Possible v3 fix:
   > The model might answer instantly: simplify the page, reduce friction, add a clearer
   > button. Some of that may help.

2. Research caveat:
   > And in human-AI studies, explanations can change reliance without reliably making
   > people better at spotting which recommendations are correct.

   Keep the claim, but speak it more plainly:
   > In some human-AI studies, explanations changed how people relied on a system. They did
   > not reliably help people spot which recommendations were correct.

3. Ending line before the final questions:
   > A good model can widen the room: more options, clearer tradeoffs, sharper questions,
   > and a second pass on your reasoning.

   This line is good enough to keep if it sounds natural in narration.

## Repetition and abstraction

Mechanical term counts in narration:

- `model`: 16
- `goal`: 14
- `answer`: 12
- `evidence`: 8
- `recommendation`: 7
- `AI`: 6
- `decision`: 5
- `values`: 4
- `tradeoff`: 4
- `prompt`: 3
- `ownership`: 3
- `grounding`: 2

This is not automatically a problem because the video teaches a frame. The most likely
spoken-rhythm risk is repeated use of **answer**, **model**, and **recommendation**. v3
should vary a few of these without making the language less clear.

Potential substitutions:

- “answer” → “first draft,” “advice,” “response” in selected places.
- “model” → “system,” “tool,” or omit when context is clear.
- “recommendation” → “advice,” “next step,” or “suggestion.”

## Scene-level notes

### Scene 1 — Cold open

Strong. The phrase “slowing the first answer down” is useful and concrete. Keep.

Possible issue: “human parts of the decision visible” is accurate but abstract. If v3 needs
more warmth, try:

> ...so you can still see what you are choosing.

### Scene 2 — Half-built question

Better than v1 because the onboarding example is shorter. It may still feel product-like,
but it now works as a quick clean example rather than the whole video.

Possible v3 cut: reduce the recommendation list from five items to three.

### Scene 3 — Grounding

Clear and practical. “Checklist for reality” remains one of the best lines. Keep.

### Scene 4 — Modest frame

The claim discipline is good, but this section still risks sounding like a citation detour.
Split the explanations sentence in v3 and keep the section under ~85 words.

### Scene 5 — Ownership

This is now the human center of the piece. The gentle/direct/firm contrast is concrete and
worth keeping.

Possible v3 improvement: after “The model can show versions,” add a short beat before the
responsibility line. Example:

> It can put the options side by side. You choose what you are willing to stand behind.

### Scene 6 — Reusable prompt

The split prompt-card decision is correct. The narration should pause after each numbered
item in the eventual recording.

### Scene 7 — How to use it

This scene improves usefulness, but it contains the longest sentence and several repeated
conditional lines. v3 should tighten this section rather than cut it.

### Scene 8 — Ending

The ending lands better after the new action-oriented scene. The final line is still a bit
poetic, but now it has enough practical setup to work.

## Recommended v3 edits

Before any render, make a v3 script that:

1. Splits the human-AI explanation sentence into two shorter sentences.
2. Cuts the onboarding advice list from five examples to three.
3. Replaces the 23-word “values, context, or risk tolerance” sentence with a two-sentence
   version.
4. Adds one short “put the options side by side” line in Ownership.
5. Keeps total narration under 1,000 words if possible.
6. Preserves every source caveat and the non-validated-intervention warning.
