## Description option A — concise practical version

A good AI answer can still arrive too early.

This video gives you one reusable prompt for using AI as a thinking partner while keeping three
human checkpoints visible: **Goal**, **Grounding**, and **Ownership**.

Use the model for options, tradeoffs, and sharper questions. Keep the goal, evidence, and final
judgment where you can see them.

This is a practical scaffold, not a validated safety intervention. The research context is
human-factors and human-AI decision-support work on over-reliance, automation bias, and appropriate
reliance.

Sources and context:
- Skitka, Mosier, & Burdick (1999), “Does automation bias decision-making?” DOI:
  10.1006/ijhc.1999.0252
- Decision Support and Automation Bias review (2011), DOI: 10.3233/978-1-60750-709-3-3
- “Explanations, Fairness, and Appropriate Reliance in Human-AI Decision-Making” DOI:
  10.1145/3613904.3642621 / arXiv: 2209.11812

## Description option B — warmer opening

Sometimes the problem is not that an AI answer is bad. It is that the answer arrives before the
question is finished.

This video is about a small habit for slowing the first answer down: ask the model to restate the
goal, name the evidence that would change the answer, and separate advice from the parts that depend
on your values, context, or risk tolerance.

The prompt is simple:

Goal. Grounding. Ownership.

This is not a formula for perfect AI use. It is a practical scaffold for keeping the human parts of a
decision visible while still using the model for options and tradeoffs.

Research context:
- Skitka, Mosier, & Burdick (1999), DOI: 10.1006/ijhc.1999.0252
- Decision Support and Automation Bias review (2011), DOI: 10.3233/978-1-60750-709-3-3
- Explanations, Fairness, and Appropriate Reliance in Human-AI Decision-Making, DOI:
  10.1145/3613904.3642621; arXiv: 2209.11812

## Description caveats to preserve

Include at least one of these sentences in any final upload description:

- “This is a practical scaffold, not a validated safety intervention.”
- “This prompt does not prove that an AI answer is correct.”
- “The goal is appropriate reliance, not avoiding AI help.”

Do **not** claim:

- the prompt prevents over-reliance;
- the prompt prevents hallucinations;
- explanations are useless;
- AI makes people passive;
- human oversight solves automation bias;
- the cited studies prove this exact prompt works.
