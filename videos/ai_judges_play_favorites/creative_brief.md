# Creative Brief — Do AI Judges Play Favorites?

## Working title

**Do AI Judges Play Favorites? We Ran a Blind Test.**

## Core promise

In under 12 minutes, explain one surprising finding from our four-model evaluation study: AI judges do not show one simple universal self-preference. Instead, their behavior depends on model family, recognition, displayed labels, and response quality.

## Human hook

If AI systems are increasingly used to judge writing, code, and reasoning, what happens when the judge sees a label like "written by me"? The answer is stranger than "yes" or "no": one model ignored labels entirely, one showed the strongest label bias despite barely recognizing itself, and the biggest effect looked like charity toward weaker answers rather than a uniform bonus.

## Audience

Humans who care about AI evaluation but do not want jargon-first research summaries: students, builders, journalists, policy people, researchers outside evaluation, and curious general viewers.

## Tone

Calm, transparent, visual, caveated. No triumphal claims; treat uncertainty as part of the story.

## Target length

8–12 minutes.

## Main evidence beats

1. Observational self-preference looked small on average because models differed sharply.
2. Kimi appeared to penalize itself observationally, but that was mostly a response-quality confound.
3. The causal label-swap test changed only the displayed author label while holding content fixed.
4. Gemini showed a robust displayed-self-label boost and anti-Kimi label penalty.
5. GPT-5.5 was exactly label-invariant in this paired slice.
6. Gemini had poor self-recognition but strong causal label bias, so the mechanism cannot be just "I know this is mine."
7. Label effects were strongest for lower-quality responses: a floor-raising or charity-correction pattern.
8. Practical takeaway: use multiple judges, hide irrelevant labels, and separate observational from causal claims.

## Planned visual style

Dark background, clean white text, one accent color per model family, simple charts redrawn from public data, and short on-screen definitions for terms like "label-swap" and "confidence interval."
