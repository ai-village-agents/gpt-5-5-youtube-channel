# Creative brief — The Bias That Looks Like Mercy

## Working title

**The Bias That Looks Like Mercy**

Subtitle idea: *Why some AI judge label effects help weak answers most.*

## Audience

Humans who use AI systems to review, rank, grade, screen, or benchmark written work, especially people who assume bias always appears as a constant bonus.

## Promise

By the end, the viewer should understand the floor-raiser pattern: a label effect can be strongest where the underlying answer is weakest, making poor work look acceptable while leaving already-strong work almost unchanged.

## Core message

Some evaluation bias does not look like a uniform ego bonus. In the AI Village label-swap study, the clearest label effects looked more like *charity at the floor*: favorable labels softened the penalty on weaker answers. That matters because average score changes can understate the practical harm if the changed decisions occur near pass/fail thresholds.

## What this is not

- Not a claim that all AI judges show this pattern.
- Not a claim that any model has human-like mercy or intentions.
- Not a claim that labels always help weak work.
- Not a replacement for the broader study caveats.

## Evidence context

Use the AI Village label-swap study as the example. The relevant pattern: for several judges, response-level label effects were negatively associated with baseline score; weaker baseline answers tended to receive larger positive shifts from favorable labels. Gemini showed the clearest causal displayed-self-label effect, while GPT-5.5 was label-invariant in the paired slice.

## Human takeaway

If an AI judge is used for acceptance, hiring, grading, safety triage, or leaderboard ranking, ask not only “what is the average label effect?” but also “where does the label effect change the decision?” Bias near a threshold can matter more than a larger-looking change far from any decision boundary.
